Particulates: Size Matters

Smaller particles with a diameter of 2.5 microns or less are likely responsible for most of the adverse health effects because they can reach the lower regions of the respiratory tract. A micron is a very tiny unit of measurement that is not visible to the human eye.

For comparison, a human hair is about 30-100 microns in width.

Particulates, Health and the AQI

Many scientific studies connect particle pollution exposure to a variety of health issues including:

  • irritation of the eyes, nose and throat
  • coughing, chest tightness and shortness of breath
  • reduced lung function
  • irregular heartbeat
  • asthma attacks
  • heart attacks
  • premature death in people with heart or lung disease

People that are most sensitive to the effects are those with existing illnesses, the elderly and children.

Source: https://airnow.gov/index.cfm?action=aqibasics.particle

Where There's Smoke, There's Fire (Upwind)

Every summer, many wildfires burn in the western United States, releasing particulates and gases that can affect air quality far downwind. Because prevailing winds in the western US tend to blow toward the east, air quality in the Grand Valley is often negatively affected by distant wildfires. Particulates from these fires can raise the AQI levels displayed on these screens.

Temperature Inversion

Normally, the atmosphere gets cooler as elevation increases. A temperature inversion occurs when cooler air is trapped under a layer of warmer air. Often, this occurs in valleys or plains surrounded by mountains. Temperature inversions often occur in the Grand Valley during winter.

Temperature inversions can have a strong negative impact on air quality. When a temperature inversion occurs, the warmer layer acts as a boundary, trapping particulates and gas emissions in the cooler air.

Citizens for Clean Air

Citizens for Clean Air is a local organization of private citizens concerned with how air quality affects the health and welfare of residents in Grand Junction and beyond.

CCA formed in January 2013 after a difficult winter in the Grand Valley with a severe air inversion that caused lingering air pollution for several months.

For more information, please visit www.citizensforcleanair.org on your mobile device or home computer.

Purple Air

PurpleAir is an organization that manufactures air quality monitors and tracks air quality all over the world. Each of the colored circles you see on this map is a PurpleAir sensor!

PurpleAir sensors use lasers to gather information on the number and size of particulates in the air. These sensors measure levels of particulates, but they do not detect the levels of different kinds of gas in the air.
